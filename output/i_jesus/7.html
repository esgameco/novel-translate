Redley had been kneeling under the statue of the deity, feeling despair, his expression as if the sky had fallen.

Yet the deity Yin found his anguish amusing and asked him, "Why do you want them to be able to see me so badly?"

Redley spread his arms, devoutly saying to the deity Yin on the altar, "How can one have faith in a deity one cannot see? How can one listen to the deity's will?"

Yin asked again, "Why should they have faith in me?"

Redley suddenly couldn't say a word, for in his view, having faith in a deity required no reason.

Yin asked again, "Or is it that you think it's important for me that they have faith in me? Or that I will cease to exist if they lose faith in me?"

"I am right here, whether they can see me or not, I am always here."

"I also have no will to promulgate to you, as long as you can multiply and create your own civilization."

Redley knelt forward until he reached Yin's feet, "We have faith in you, not because you need us, but because we need you."

"It's like..."

He pondered for a long time but couldn't find the right descriptive words.

Until he saw the ocean and sun outside through the temple's pillars.

"It's like! Life needs water, needs light."

Yin smiled slightly. As an atheist, even if the true creator deity was standing before him now, he would only see it as a powerful higher life form.

His concept of a deity was completely different from Redley's concept.

His expression was one of neither affirmation nor denial, disdainful of the word "deity", even though he himself was called one.

The Redley below him, however, was so steadfast.

But what Redley said next moved Yin.

He placed his head under the altar, against Yin's feet, tears in his eyes.

His tone became solemn, his voice somewhat sad.

"Lord Yin, what I fear most is that if I die one day, who will serve you in this temple, who will guard this temple until the end of time?"

"If even my children cannot see you, then how can this continue?"

"You are so supremely exalted, you can bestow wisdom and life upon all things, you can..."

"Enough to change this desolate and lifeless world, yet unable to change one's own loneliness."

Yinshen looked at him.

He originally wanted to say, this is just your own thought.

I don't need anyone to guard this huge tomb mound, nor will I stay in this temple until the end of time. One day I will eventually leave. What you are guarding is not me, but merely a stone.

But seeing Liedelijī's tears, he suddenly softened for some reason.

"Then what do you want!"

Liedelijī wiped away his tears and looked up at Yinshen.

"I want to create a great civilization, a civilization that will never decline."

"I want them to worship You forever, forever surrounding this temple below."

"I want to make an everlasting vow with all the Trifolians, so that they will guard this palace generation after generation, engraving their belief in You into their bloodlines."

Yinshen shook his head at the still naive Liedelijī.

"These things only have meaning for you, they are meaningless to me."

"There is no eternal civilization, even the sun in the sky will eventually burn out, the universe is inevitably heading towards destruction."

"No matter how great a civilization, it cannot withstand the erosion of time."

"Even the most devout belief will be forgotten, and the tallest and hardest temples and statues will ultimately collapse into ruins."

Liedelijī did not understand what this meant, only feeling that these words penetrated through the ages and eternity, a mystery he could not reach.

Yinshen stroked the bone helmet on his head, his voice not as calm and hollow as usual.

"But if you are willing to do it, then go ahead!"

"If these things are what you consider the meaning of life."

Liedelijī finally said, "If one day I die, please God, take something off my body and keep it by Your side."

"This way!"

"I will be able to accompany God forever."

Yinshen watched Liedelijī's back as he left the temple, and finally felt that Liedelijī was no longer an insect, a thing he had casually created.

He was a living life, a person with feelings.

Hundreds became thousands now.

This island finally had a semblance of civilization and the appearance of a city-state.

The Trifolians understood reverence. Civilization had a distribution of power. They did not dare call Leadri's name, instead calling him the Wise King, and referring to this city as the God-Given City.

Meaning, everything was a gift from the divine.

A palace was built in the valley behind the pyramid, where Leadri and his children lived.

Leadri appointed his sons to various positions in the God-Given City, some in charge of construction, some overseeing food and warehouses, etc.

They held power and unified the thousands of Trifolians, who originally were equal but now divided into different classes.

This Trifolian kingdom or city-state began to have a simple power structure.

Leadri was the king, his sons the first generation of Trifolian nobles.

One day, Leadri's second son suddenly ran in and knelt before him, his face full of panic.

"Great Wise King, more have been swallowed by the sea."

Leadri sat on the cold stone throne, looking at his second son.

"How many?"

"The entire squad never returned."

Leadri stood up, somewhat worried.

He was not just worried that people had died, but the hidden crisis behind it.

Being "swallowed by the sea" referred to those who entered the deep sea and never returned, most never coming back.

This was not the first time.

As the Trifolian population grew, the food from catching insects and harvesting sea slugs was no longer enough, even as they tried drying sea plants as food, it was still inadequate.

Their massive size for that era, thousands gathered on the island, was enough to deplete the surrounding sea, and there could be no food on land.

Their appearance was too abrupt for this world, which was not yet prepared to receive Trifolian civilization.

People in the God-Given City began starving to death. They had to venture into the deep sea, which was far from the tranquility they imagined.

The ocean could give birth to life, but also devour it.

Many Trifolians were lost in the deep sea, trapped in dangerous areas, or encountered sudden situations and never returned.

Leadri knew the Trifolian civilization faced its first crisis.
